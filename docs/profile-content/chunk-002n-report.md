# Chunk 2N Report

## Scope

Chunk `2N` is a mixed category-repair pass built around the strongest currently sourceable non-plant backlog instead of another volume-driven weed cluster. It adds `8` curated profiles and raises the curated total from `294` to `302`.

Profiles added in this batch:

- Bagrada Bug
- Cactus Moth
- Polyphagous Shot Hole Borer
- Asian Tiger Shrimp
- Asian Swamp Eel
- Vermiculated Sailfin Catfish
- Brown Hoplo
- European Larch Canker

## Contradictions Resolved

The main contradiction after `2M` was whether to keep chasing the recent `30+` pace even though the remaining mixed backlog still does not have a comparably dense official source ecosystem.

This chunk resolves that directly:

- `2N` returns to a smaller mixed pass
- it favors category repair across insects, wildlife, and fungi-diseases
- it does not pretend the non-plant backlog is suddenly deep enough for another quality-safe `20 to 30` pass

There was also a second contradiction around what counts as source-ready outside the plant backlog:

- some wildlife and insect candidates still have county coverage and images but weak public-facing guidance
- others have narrower geography but much stronger official action language
- this pass chose the latter when the writing needed a clearer report, quarantine, or do-not-move path

## What Improved

- The catalog picked up three new insect profiles that have clearer public signals than many of the remaining insect backups: crop and garden damage for `Bagrada Bug`, quarantine and cactus movement for `Cactus Moth`, and tree-dieback reporting for `Polyphagous Shot Hole Borer`.
- Wildlife coverage improved through fish and crustacean profiles that finally move beyond the repeated feral-swine pattern. `Asian Tiger Shrimp`, `Asian Swamp Eel`, `Vermiculated Sailfin Catfish`, and `Brown Hoplo` all add distinct pathways and habitats instead of generic mammal nuisance framing.
- The fungi-disease category gained a regulated forest-health profile in `European Larch Canker`, which helps category balance without forcing a weak home-treatment narrative.

## Weak Spots In This Batch

- Several profiles in this chunk still depend on surveillance and reporting logic more than on household removal. That is accurate, but it naturally makes the action sections less hands-on than the strongest weed profiles.
- `Brown Hoplo` and `Vermiculated Sailfin Catfish` are useful additions, but their best practical public guidance still comes from general nonnative-reporting workflows layered onto species-specific NAS factsheets.
- The chunk improves category balance, but it does not prove the next mixed pass can safely jump to `20 to 30` species without either a new source cluster or a parallel research effort to strengthen the remaining backlog.

## Process Lessons

- Mixed category repair is still viable when the shortlist is kept honest. The real danger is not a smaller count but pretending thin candidates are ready because the user wants scale.
- Official aquatic and wildlife source stacks can support more profiles than the current catalog shows, but they often need a second source for public action language rather than for identification.
- If the project wants repeated `20 to 30` mixed-category chunks, the bottleneck is now source ecosystem depth, not willingness to draft.

## Recommendation For The Next Batch

1. Treat `2N` as the model for the next non-plant pass: smaller, mixed, and source-disciplined.
2. Build a research-first backup pool before promising another `20 to 30` mixed chunk.
3. Run the state and county data-audit track in parallel with content drafting, because stronger local reporting sources may improve both profile selection and wildlife coverage.
