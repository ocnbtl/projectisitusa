# Chunk 2J Report

## Scope

Chunk `2J` is a targeted follow-through on the `2I` backup pool rather than another scale-up pass. It adds `7` curated profiles and raises the curated total from `220` to `227`.

Profiles added in this batch:

- Introduced Pine Sawfly
- Spruce Aphid
- Black Imported Fire Ant
- Egyptian Goose
- Black Spiny-tailed Iguana
- Rosary Pea
- European Alder

## Contradictions Resolved

The main contradiction after `2I` was whether the backup pool should be treated like a deferred mini-batch or just a holding bin.

This batch resolves that in favor of a smaller, source-clean pass:

- The stronger `2I` source bar still held.
- The batch stayed intentionally small instead of padding toward a round number.
- Backup-pool species moved only when the public action path and naming cleanup were strong enough.

One naming contradiction was also explicit and worth fixing:

- `Elatobium abietinum` was still carrying the registry common name `Green spruce aphid`.
- Forest Service material makes clear that North American usage prefers `Spruce Aphid`, while `green spruce aphid` can refer to a different species.
- The curated entry now uses `Spruce Aphid` instead of repeating the weaker registry label.

Another public-facing cleanup was necessary:

- `Abrus precatorius` still surfaced as `Rosarypea` in generated data.
- The curated profile now uses `Rosary Pea`, which is the clearer and better-supported public name.

## What Improved

- The batch keeps category repair moving without pretending every remaining backup species is ready.
- Public-facing naming improved in a substantive way, not just cosmetically. `Spruce Aphid`, `Introduced Pine Sawfly`, `Rosary Pea`, and `European Alder` all read more clearly than their raw registry labels.
- The wildlife entries avoided generic aquarium-release copy. `Egyptian Goose` and `Black Spiny-tailed Iguana` now have action language tied to actual conflict types instead of boilerplate.
- The plant tail stayed distinct. `Rosary Pea` is framed first as a toxic invasive vine, while `European Alder` is framed as a water-moving wetland tree problem, not another generic woody invader.

## Weak Spots In This Batch

- `Egyptian Goose` still has a state-by-state action problem. The risk framing is strong, but actual take rules vary and needed conservative wording.
- `Black Imported Fire Ant` remains harder to separate cleanly from hybrid and red imported fire ant language than an ideal standalone profile would allow.
- `Black Spiny-tailed Iguana` still has a Florida-heavy source ecosystem. That is defensible because the established U.S. context is Florida-heavy, but it remains a narrower source base than some other wildlife entries.

## Process Lessons

- The backup pool works better as a pressure-release valve than as a promise. It gives the project a next move without forcing weak species into the queue.
- Naming cleanup keeps mattering because a few registry labels are still actively misleading for public readers, not just awkward.
- Small mixed batches are still productive after a bigger pass. They help preserve distinctness and let the source ledger stay honest.

## Recommendation For The Next Batch

1. Keep treating the remaining backup pool as optional, not owed.
2. Continue favoring species where the public name needs improvement and the source base clearly supports the correction.
3. Look next at the remaining wildlife and plant backups only if their action language can stay as specific as it is here.
