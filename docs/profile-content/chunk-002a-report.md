# Chunk 2A Report

## Scope

Chunk 2A was executed as a 15-profile pilot batch inside the broader 100-profile tranche.

Profiles added or upgraded in this batch:

- Japanese Beetle
- Brown Marmorated Stink Bug
- Spongy Moth
- Spotted-wing Drosophila
- Red Imported Fire Ant
- Dutch Elm Disease
- Chestnut Blight
- Multiflora Rose
- Japanese Honeysuckle
- Cheatgrass
- Field Bindweed
- Garlic Mustard
- Purple Loosestrife
- Zebra Mussel
- Common Carp

## Contradiction Resolved

The earlier Chunk 2A planning document said `25` profiles but only listed `24` species.

That contradiction was not smoothed over. Instead, the current batch was explicitly reset as a 15-profile pilot based on source readiness, public relevance, and category balance.

## What Improved

- The workflow handled a much larger batch than Chunk 1 without breaking validation or the build.
- The source inventory now captures every new source used in the same pass as the profile writing.
- The new batch broadens the profile set beyond forest pests into aquatic invasives, home nuisance pests, wetland plants, and agricultural pests.
- The action guidance is becoming more consistent about when to use `diy`, `report`, or `both`.

## What Worked Well

- APHIS pages remained strong for invasive insect profiles with movement restrictions or reporting components.
- University extension pages were especially useful for practical `What to do` guidance.
- National Park Service pages worked well for invasive plants because they often combine ID, impact, and concrete control notes in one place.
- UMN Extension was especially useful for species where the public action path is identify, contain, and report.

## Weak Spots In This Batch

- Some species still rely on one dominant source page instead of a richer pair of primary references.
- Chestnut blight and cheatgrass are accurate and useful, but they are more general-public profiles than treatment-specific ones.
- The current pilot still leans heavily toward plants, because those species had the best combination of image readiness, public relevance, and practical extension guidance.

## Process Lessons

- Scaling is possible, but only when source logging happens inline with drafting.
- Public relevance should keep outweighing raw county coverage when choosing the next batch.
- Extension pages often provide the best public action language, even when a federal page is better for origin or quarantine context.
- Wildlife and aquatic species tend to produce cleaner `report` or `both` guidance than aggressive `diy` guidance, which helps keep recommendations conservative.

## Recommendation For The Next Batch

The next step should not jump straight to another arbitrary 25 if the source mix is thin.

Instead:

1. Reuse the current source institutions where possible.
2. Add the next 15 to 20 profiles from the deferred Chunk 2A list.
3. Keep a balanced mix of insects, diseases, plants, and wildlife.
4. Prefer species with clear public action paths and at least one good official or extension source before drafting.

That keeps the workflow scalable while still moving meaningfully toward larger tranche sizes.
